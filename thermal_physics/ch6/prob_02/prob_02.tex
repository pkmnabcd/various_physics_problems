\begin{flushleft}

We begin by recalling that a fundamental assumption of statistical mechanics is that degenerate microstates are equally likely.
So, for a given energy $E(s)$, if there are $n$ microstates within that macrostate and the probability of each microstate has probabilty $\mathcal{P}(s)$, then the probability of the atom having energy $E$ is $\mathcal{P}(E)=n\mathcal{P}(s)$.\\~\\

From the 2nd law of thermodynamics, we know that entropy of a system's state $S=k\ln(\Omega)$, where $\Omega$ is the multiplicity of the particular state.
Here, $n$ is the multiplicity, so we can replace $\Omega$ with $n$ and solve for $n$ so that we can plug in that expression into what we found above.\\~\\

$$S=k\ln(n)$$
$$\implies \frac{S}{k}=\ln(n)$$
$$\implies e^{(S/k)}=n$$\\~\\

So, this implies that
$$\mathcal{P}(E)=n\mathcal{P}(s)=e^{(S/k)}\mathcal{P}(s)$$
We also know that the probability of a microstate is according to the Boltzmann distribution:
$$\mathcal{P}(s)=\frac{1}{Z}e^{-E(s)/kT}$$
Plugging this into our equation for $\mathcal{P}(E)$, we get
$$\implies\mathcal{P}(E)=e^{(S/k)}\frac{1}{Z}e^{-E/kT}$$
$$\implies\mathcal{P}(E)=\frac{1}{Z}e^{(S/k)-E/kT}$$
$$\implies\mathcal{P}(E)=\frac{1}{Z}e^{-(E-TS)/kT}$$
Substituting $F=E-TS$, this gives the final result:
$$\implies\mathcal{P}(E)=\frac{1}{Z}e^{-F/kT}$$




\end{flushleft}
